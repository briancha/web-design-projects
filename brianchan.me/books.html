Books reading list and recommendations

I try to read whenever I have a chance. I firmly believe that:

A person who won't read has no advantage over one who can't read.

Mark Twain
My favorite books are at the top, followed by the books I've finished most recently. This list was inspired by John Collison's reading list. Books I recommend are in bold.
Note: the length of the description does not correlate with how much I enjoyed the book.

All-time favorites

How to Win Friends and Influence People

Every time I am in a social situation, whether meeting someone for the first time or talking to a lifelong friend, I use principles from this book. The principles are so simple—listen, remember people's names—but so difficult to train yourself to implement.

Too often we spend so much time talking about ourselves that we completely ignore others' need to express their opinions and feel important.

I truly believe that internalizing the principles of this book will give you the equivalent of ten years of social experience.

The Hard Thing About Hard Things

Ben Horowitz is brutally honest about the challenges he faced in starting, then being CEO of Loudcloud (later Opsware). It shows the incredible demands of being CEO of a public company. Must-read for any aspiring entrepreneur.

Getting Past No: Negotiating With Difficult People

Most of us think we're pretty good at negotiation. We'll walk into a dealership demanding the price we want, threatening to walk away at any time. But what if we're negotiating from a position of weakness? What if walking away is not an option?

This book is to negotiation what How to Win Friends and Influence People is to social interaction. Via Tim Ferriss.

The Fish That Ate The Whale

The American Dream: how a poor Jewish immigrant Sam Zemurray became one of the wealthiest men in America via Ryan Holiday. Along the way, Zemurray overthrew Central American governments, turned bananas from an exotic fruit to a staple (the bestselling item at Walmart today), and controlled a 68% share of the banana market at one point.

Rich Cohen is probably the best storyteller I have ever come across.

2015

Mistakes Were Made (But Not By Me)

Often, we look at criminals, dictators, and disgraced politicians, and think, "How do they live with themselves?" The answer lies in the human ability of self-justification. As humans, we are capable of justifying nearly any action, as long as it happens one small step at a time. We convince ourselves of our own goodness by altering our memories, blaming others, and insisting on being right. Once we form an opinion, it is very hard for us to change it. This causes small errors to turn into lifelong mistakes. Insightful read.

Essentialism: The Disciplined Pursuit of Less

The basic principle of this book is that if you don't prioritize your life, someone else will (and not in the way that you wanted). Say no to everything where you cannot make your highest contribution. Via Noah Kagan.

The 33 Strategies of War

Over time, war has gradually become more indirect, subtle, asymmetric, and unconventional. In war, oftentimes, the more adaptable, maneuverable side with fewer resources wins.

The Social Animal

Through narratives about two people, David Brooks contrasts life as a child growing up in the middle class compared with the poor. Throughout, Brooks includes several interesting facts, for example:

  • Marital satisfaction follows U-shaped curve: highest in the first few years, declines and bottoms out when children are teens, and climbs back up in retirement
  • Babies are cute so their mothers will take care of them
  • Self-control is 2x as important as IQ in predicting high school performance, school attendance, and final grades.

Book of Investing Wisdom

I specifically recommend the essay by Peter Lynch: Stalking the Tenbagger (a chapter from his book One Up On Wall Street). It completely changed my thinking and the way I approach investing.

  • Summary: Invest in what you know: companies that you interact with on a daily basis (work for, buy from, or sell to). You have an "inside" advantage on these companies because you know more about them than the average Wall Street analyst does. You can see trends up to a year ahead of financial analysts.
  • After reading this, I added a rule to my investing checklist: do I buy from, work for, or sell to this company in my daily life?

10% Happier

To be happier and calmer, meditate and live in the present. I strive to do both every day.

How Will You Measure Your Life?

What I love most about this book is that Clay applies the business theories he has worked on throughout his career to people's personal lives, giving business examples of his concepts along the way.

Too often, ambitious people invest their time and energy into their careers, to the detriment of their families and relationships. They don't often realize this until the end of their lives, when they have estranged families and few close relationships.

The moral of the story: be as deliberate with your life as you would be with your career or company.

The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing

Better to dominate a small pond instead of being a bit player in a big pond. Focus whenever possible. Via Tim Ferriss.

The One Minute Manager

How to become one:

  1. Define your direct report's responsibilities as clearly as possible. Make sure both manager and employee agree on them.
  2. Early on, catch employees doing something right. This assures the employee that the manager is on their side.
  3. When an employee makes a mistake, be tough on the behavior, but supportive of the person.
Via Jason Womack.

The Innovator's Dilemma

Disruptive technologies often have lower profit margins than existing businesses, i.e. digital cameras compared with Kodak's film business. Thus, the dilemma: should a company invest in a disruptive technology and potentially kill its own profit margins and existing business? Via Aaron Levie.

Mindset

Contrasts the difference in outcomes between having a fixed mindset (belief that basic traits are fixed) and growth mindset (belief that traits can be developed through effort and deliberate application).

Dweck's TED talk provides a good overview of the perils of the fixed mindset and benefits of the growth mindset:

Lean In For Graduates

Same content as Lean In, except it includes tips on resumes and interviews.

Mastery

Excellent book on how to achieve mastery: focus, passion, years of investment in a certain field and vocation.

Disclosure: big fan of Robert Greene.

Becoming Steve Jobs

Unofficial biography. I agree with Jobs' closest friends and family that it paints a more nuanced portrait of him compared with Isaacson's book.

Bold

Expands on the exponential potential of Moore's Law and using that potential to build the companies of the future (through channels such as crowdsourcing and crowdfunding.

Recession-Proof Graduate

Don't use conventional job hunting strategies. Instead, build your online presence, and do free work for someone you admire. Inspired the very site you are looking at.

The 48 Laws of Power

Guidebook for navigating a treacherous world.

I am still young and have little work experience. In many ways, I have a naive perspective of the world. Books like this one are crucial to my better understanding human nature.
